Option 01

Circular Seal

A monogram set inside a thin double-ring — the visual language of a chartered institution, a university crest, a private trust. The "C" is set in display weight and the "b" is set in italic accent script, so the mark itself contains the brand's two type voices.

Why it feels premium: Restraint. Two thin lines and a piece of typography. No gradient, no fill, no icon. Reads as serious and old-money without being stuffy.

Option 02

Script-Accent Wordmark

Pure wordmark, no icon. "Claude" is set in your accent script, "Buzizi" in display serif — the two voices already in your brand, locked together. A hand-drawn rule traces underneath, then "Foundation" in tight uppercase tracking.

Why it feels premium: Reads like the masthead of a magazine or a handwritten signature on a charter. The script is the difference between corporate and personal.

Option 03

Editorial Stack

Three layers of typography stacked: tracked uppercase eyebrow ("THE"), display-serif name, hairline rule, tracked uppercase footer ("FOUNDATION"). Built from rules and type only — no symbol.

Why it feels premium: This is the visual grammar of Vanity Fair, the New York Public Library, and the Aspen Institute. Pure typography, no decoration. The rule between lines does the work.

Option 04

Lifting Sigil

A custom sigil — two arcs forming the silhouette of a lifted hand, a leaf, or a doorway. The dot inside reads as the person being lifted. Paired with a single-line wordmark and your three pillars as a tracked subtitle.

Why it feels premium: A custom mark elevates the brand beyond pure typography. The geometry is ambiguous on purpose — it works as a hand, a flame, a sail. Every viewer projects their own meaning.

Option 05

Heraldic Crest

A shield-form crest holding the CB monogram, with three dots representing Feed, Educate, and Equip. Paired with the wordmark, a founding-year line, and the three pillars in script accent.

Why it feels premium: Borrows the visual authority of family foundations and old institutions (Rockefeller, Ford, Mellon). Says "we mean to be here for a hundred years." Use sparingly — this is the most formal of the six.

Option 06

Editorial Masthead

The full name laid out as a single horizontal masthead, with the middle word ("Buzizi") set in script accent. Bracketed top and bottom by hairline rules and tracked details — the founding year above, the three pillars below.

Why it feels premium: This is a newspaper nameplate, a benefit-dinner invitation, a stationery letterhead. Works beautifully on a single line at the top of any document. Use this when the logo gets to be the design (covers, posters, plaques).

My recommendation

Option 02 (Script-Accent Wordmark) for the brand. Option 01 (Circular Seal) as a favicon / app icon companion.

The script-accent wordmark gives the brand its personal voice without becoming a logo-with-a-face. The circular seal gives you the small-format mark you need wherever the wordmark won't fit (favicon, social avatar, embossed plaques). Together they cover every situation a foundation actually needs.
