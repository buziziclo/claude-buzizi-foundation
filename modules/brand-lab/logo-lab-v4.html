Logo Lab — Round 4

Wordmark-led. The name is the logo.

Reset. The strongest foundation logos — Ford, MacArthur, Hewlett, Open Society — lead with the wordmark and let the mark stay quiet. Round 3 had it backwards.

Six directions below. Each one is shown at real working size in four contexts: light header, dark footer, larger hero stamp, and a favicon-only crop. Decide on the system, not the artwork — pick the typographic voice first.

Option 01

Archivo + Sun Mark

Archivo Black for the name (institutional, modern, confident), small Rwandan-sun mark to the left. Heavy + small mark = serious nonprofit. Color split shown.

Option 02

Archivo + Hairline Rule

No mark. Wordmark-only with a thin hairline rule between "CLAUDE BUZIZI" and "FOUNDATION." Most premium-feeling — the grammar of Ford Foundation, MacArthur, Hewlett. Lets the name carry everything.

Option 04

Fraunces Serif + Rule

Fraunces (display serif) for the name, set in caps. Reads as established institution: museum, university press, foundation of record. Same hairline-rule treatment as Option 02. The orange becomes a literary accent, not a brand shout.

Option 06

Tag Bar + Wordmark

A solid orange vertical bar (a "tag" or "spine") sits to the left of the wordmark. Mechanical, editorial, very small footprint. The bar is the brand mark — no glyph, no figure. Most distinctive at favicon size.

My recommendation

Option 02 (Archivo + Hairline Rule) — wordmark-only, the grammar of every great foundation.

It's the most legible at every size, ages best, and lets the brand carry meaning through the name and the work — not through a competing glyph. The hairline rule is the only mark, and it does what every great mark does: it disappears until you need it.
